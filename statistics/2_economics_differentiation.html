Using Differentiation to Optimize Business Decisions

üìö The Business Problem

A company sells a product. They know their total revenue based on how many units they sell, but they want to know: "Should we produce one more unit?"

To answer this, they need to find the marginal revenue - the additional revenue from selling one more unit.

This is a perfect job for differentiation!

Revenue Function: R(q) = 20q - 0.5q¬≤
Total Revenue ($)
Marginal Revenue ($/unit)

üéØ What's Happening?

Green Curve (Total Revenue): Shows total money earned from selling q units. Notice it increases, peaks, then would decline!

Orange Line (Marginal Revenue): Shows the additional revenue from selling one more unit. This is the derivative!

Taking the Derivative:

MR(q) = d/dq[R(q)] = d/dq[20q - 0.5q¬≤]

MR(q) = 20 - q

Key Business Insights:

  • At q = 0 units, marginal revenue = $20 (first unit brings in $20)
  • At q = 10 units, marginal revenue = $10 (10th unit adds $10)
  • At q = 20 units, marginal revenue = $0 (revenue is maximized!)
  • Beyond q = 20, marginal revenue becomes negative (losing money per unit)

Every business uses marginal analysis (differentiation) to make decisions:

üßÆ The Mathematics

Differentiation Rules Used:

  • Power Rule: d/dq[q¬≤] = 2q
  • Constant Multiple: d/dq[0.5q¬≤] = 0.5 √ó 2q = q
  • Linear Term: d/dq[20q] = 20
  • Sum Rule: Combine all terms
R(q) = 20q - 0.5q¬≤
‚Üì (differentiate)
MR(q) = 20 - q

Finding the Maximum Revenue:

When marginal revenue = 0, total revenue is maximized:

MR(q) = 0
20 - q = 0
q = 20 units

At 20 units, total revenue = $200 (the peak of the green curve)

üéì Key Takeaway

Marginal Analysis = Optimization through Differentiation

In economics and business, "marginal" always means "derivative." Whether it's marginal cost, marginal revenue, or marginal profit, we're using calculus to find the optimal point where the business should operate.

This is how businesses maximize profit!
